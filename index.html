Case Study

Making Interactive Publishing Operable at Scale

Context
Publishing Model PDF flipbook
Objective Interactive digital
Constraints No dev team
Budget €4,000
Ownership End-to-end

The Problem

Digital publishing existed within the organization, but only as static PDF-based editions delivered through a flipbook-style platform. While this supported distribution, it did not support interactivity. Data visualization, animated elements, and responsive content had never been produced within the existing publishing model.

Leadership wanted to explore richer digital experiences, but doing so would require workflows, tooling, and standards that did not yet exist. Without a system, any attempt at interactivity would be fragile, specialist-dependent, and difficult to sustain under live publishing deadlines.

Although a full website redesign, which would open up the possibility of a more bespoke digital publishing system, was planned for a later phase, leadership needed a short-term solution that could operate within the current infrastructure. With a constrained budget of approximately €4,000, I was responsible for defining what was feasible, owning resourcing decisions and delivering outcomes for the digital transformation work described below.

The Decision

The constraint was not technical capability, but operational reliability.

Interactive output could not depend on specialist knowledge if it was going to survive scale, turnover, and delivery pressure.

The decision was to redesign digital publishing as a system — one that encoded standards by default, reduced dependency on individual expertise, and held together as digital output increased. Rather than expanding the team or introducing complex tooling, the system itself would absorb complexity and enforce consistency.

The Intervention

With live publishing deadlines, no engineering roadmap, and no in-house development team, I took ownership of digital publishing delivery. I scoped and staffed the minimum technical support required, made tooling decisions that reduced long-term vendor dependency, and shifted production capability upstream.

Instead of relying on expensive software or specialist workflows, editors and designers could assemble interactive digital books using familiar inputs such as print PDFs — with the system handling structure, validation, and output.

Stewardship

Patterns of Leadership

Designing systems that support teams long after decisions are made

Leadership at scale isn't expressed through constant intervention. It shows up in the systems that quietly guide teams when you're not in the room.

Across roles and organizations, I've focused on building structures that reduce ambiguity, surface priorities, and help teams coordinate without friction.

These aren't one-off tools. They're patterns.

Patterns
01 Shared Reality 02 Standards at Speed 03 Embedded Accountability 04 Stewardship Over Control
01

Shared reality over status updates

The Project Master

Problem

Before the Project Master existed, progress lived in spreadsheets, meetings, and personal updates. Executives depended on me to translate scattered signals into decisions.

Intervention

I designed a single system that defined what "progress" meant, using standardized categories and weighted completion thresholds, so anyone could see where a project stood at a glance. Once a project reached a verified level of readiness, production could begin without debate or false starts.

Outcome

The result was visibility and alignment without escalation. Clear readiness thresholds reduced anxiety during high-pressure cycles and removed last-minute heroics from the process. New contributors could work with confidence, without waiting for approval or constant clarification.

02

Standards at speed

The Project Master

Problem

Flexibility is useful until it obscures risk.

Intervention

To prevent premature production and downstream delays, I made certain decisions non-negotiable. Projects could only advance once critical dependencies were met. Timelines were allowed to shift, but readiness had to be provable.

Outcome

This removed the temptation to "start anyway" and protected teams from rework, last-minute scrambles, and stalled launches.

03

Embedded accountability

The automated Gantt chart

Problem

As teams grew and new journalists were onboarded, relying on ad hoc guidance no longer worked. Expectations needed to be visible without intervention.

Intervention

I standardized the project lifecycle through automated Gantt timelines embedded directly into project trackers. Responsibilities, handoffs, and deadlines were no longer inferred — they were explicit.

Outcome

Editors spent less time orienting contributors. New team members could anticipate their work without special attention. Ownership became clearer without additional process.

04

Stewardship over control

Not everyone adopted these systems immediately. In some cases, leaders preferred personal updates over shared tools.

I held the line by redirecting decisions back to the system and explaining the long-term value of shared visibility. Over time, ad hoc updates gave way to a common understanding of progress, reducing status checks, surfacing risk earlier, and lowering escalation to me.

Stewardship meant maintaining the integrity of the system even when it would have been easier to intervene.

The result wasn't more process. It was calmer teams, clearer ownership, and more room for creative judgment under pressure.

These systems didn't eliminate complexity. They made it navigable.

They allowed work to move forward without constant clarification, protected teams from avoidable friction, and ensured that progress didn't depend on any one person being present.

That's stewardship.

Leverage

Not all work carries the same weight.

In complex organizations, effort is everywhere. Impact is harder to find.

Leverage comes from recognizing where a small structural decision can remove friction across teams and acting there instead of adding process, meetings, or headcount.

I architect systems for moments when growth, complexity, and creative ambition collide. My role was not to increase throughput at any cost, but to protect delivery quality and organizational trust over time.

The work I'm most proud of is often invisible. It shows up when teams can move forward without stopping to ask, clarify, or escalate because the system already supports the decision.

That's leverage.
